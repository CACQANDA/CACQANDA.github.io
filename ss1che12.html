Carbon and its Compounds

Objectives

Understand the chemical properties and behavior of carbon

Learn about the different forms of carbon and their uses

Explore the importance of carbon compounds in living organisms and industry

Terms

Carbon: a chemical element with the symbol C and atomic number 6

Organic compounds: compounds that contain carbon-hydrogen bonds

Hydrocarbons: organic compounds made of only carbon and hydrogen atoms

Functional groups: specific groups of atoms that determine the chemical properties of an organic compound

Carbon is a chemical element with the symbol C and atomic number 6. It is a non-metal and is the fourth most abundant element in the universe. Carbon is unique in that it can form a wide variety of chemical compounds, including both inorganic and organic compounds.

Organic compounds are compounds that contain carbon-hydrogen bonds. They are called "organic" because they were originally thought to be produced only by living organisms. However, it is now known that organic compounds can also be produced by non-biological processes. Hydrocarbons are a type of organic compound made of only carbon and hydrogen atoms. They are the primary component of fossil fuels such as coal, oil and natural gas.

Functional groups are specific groups of atoms that determine the chemical properties of an organic compound. Examples of functional groups include alcohols, carboxylic acids, and amines. These functional groups give organic compounds their unique properties and reactivity, and are essential for many chemical reactions in living organisms and industry.

Carbon compounds play a crucial role in many industrial and commercial applications. For example, hydrocarbons are used as fuel sources and as raw materials for the production of plastics and other synthetic materials. Carbon compounds are also important in the pharmaceutical industry, as many drugs contain organic compounds. In living organisms, carbon compounds such as carbohydrates, lipids, and nucleic acids are essential for life.
